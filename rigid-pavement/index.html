The Sonoran Desert doesn't compromise, and neither can a concrete pavement placed over the clay-rich alluvial deposits that characterize Tempe, Arizona. With summer pavement surface temperatures exceeding 150°F and nighttime lows dropping sharply during winter months in this part of Maricopa County, the thermal gradient through a rigid slab becomes the primary design control — far more critical than in coastal or temperate climates. In our experience, a standard AASHTO 93 rigid pavement design that works in Phoenix's central corridor often requires recalibration for Tempe's specific subgrade, particularly where the stiff, desiccated upper crust overlies softer, moisture-sensitive clays in the Salt River floodplain. We supplement the mechanistic-empirical approach with a detailed subgrade characterization, including CBR testing for road subgrade to define the modulus of subgrade reaction under seasonal moisture variation, and in-situ permeability tests where drainage through the subbase becomes critical to prevent pumping at transverse joints.

In Tempe's desert climate, curling stresses from diurnal thermal gradients often exceed traffic-induced edge stresses — the slab thickness must be governed by both, not just one.

Scope of work in Tempe Arizona

A recent industrial park expansion near the Loop 101 and Broadway Road intersection illustrates the local challenge well. The site investigation revealed a profile typical of Tempe's basin-fill geology: a thin layer of silty sand overlying fat clay with a plasticity index above 30, extending to depths of 12 feet before transitioning to caliche-cemented conglomerate. The owner initially assumed a standard 6-inch doweled slab would suffice, but the combination of high-plasticity clay and heavy truck traffic from logistics operations demanded a more rigorous analysis. Our team ran the AASHTOWare Pavement ME Design model using local climate data from the Tempe ASU weather station, which showed that a 7.5-inch slab with a 4-inch cement-treated base would be necessary to keep transverse cracking below the 10% threshold over a 30-year design life. We also specified 1.25-inch-diameter epoxy-coated dowels spaced at 12 inches to handle the load transfer across contraction joints under daily thermal expansion cycles. For projects with similar heavy industrial loading in the East Valley, we often recommend pairing the rigid pavement analysis with a flexible pavement design alternative for access roads and parking areas where asphalt proves more cost-effective over low-volume lanes.

Risks and considerations in Tempe Arizona

ASCE 7-22 and the AASHTO mechanistic-empirical design framework make explicit reference to climatic inputs, and in Tempe those inputs are unforgiving. The greatest risk we encounter is not structural failure but functional distress: joint spalling, mid-panel cracking from curling-and-warping fatigue, and loss of load transfer efficiency as the subgrade beneath the slab edges erodes during the brief but intense monsoon season between July and September. When a rigid pavement is designed without accounting for the bimodal rainfall pattern and the expansive nature of Tempe's clay subgrade, water infiltration through unsealed joints can reduce the effective k-value by 40% or more within five years. This leads to corner breaks that propagate rapidly under repeated axle loads. Our design protocols always include a sensitivity analysis for reduced subgrade support, and we specify positive drainage with daylighted subbase layers wherever site grading permits.

Quick answers

What is the typical cost range for a rigid pavement design package for a commercial project in Tempe?

For a complete design package covering subgrade investigation, pavement structural analysis, joint detailing, and construction specifications, fees typically range from US$1,820 to US$6,770 depending on project complexity, traffic loading, and the extent of geotechnical testing required. A small parking lot design falls at the lower end, while a heavily trafficked arterial or industrial yard with ME Design modeling and drainage analysis reaches the upper range.

How does Tempe's clay soil affect rigid pavement performance?

The high-plasticity clays common in the Tempe basin are susceptible to significant volume change with moisture fluctuation. During the dry summer months, desiccation shrinkage can reduce subgrade support at slab edges, while monsoon infiltration can cause swelling and loss of bearing capacity. This differential movement is the primary cause of pumping, faulting, and corner breaks in rigid pavements that lack a properly designed cement-treated base and positive drainage.

Do you use the AASHTO 93 method or the newer ME Design for Tempe projects?

We use both, selecting the appropriate method based on project scope and owner requirements. For most commercial and industrial projects, the AASHTO 93 method provides a reliable and well-accepted framework. For major arterial roadways or facilities with high traffic volumes and complex loading, we apply the AASHTOWare Pavement ME Design software using local climate files from the Tempe area, which allows us to predict distresses like transverse cracking, joint faulting, and IRI over the design life with greater accuracy.

What joint spacing do you recommend for unreinforced concrete pavements in Tempe?

We typically specify joint spacing between 12 and 15 feet for unreinforced slabs, following ACI 330 and local practice. The exact spacing depends on slab thickness, concrete coefficient of thermal expansion, and subgrade friction. In Tempe's high-temperature environment, narrower joint spacing helps reduce curling stresses and mid-panel cracking risk, though it increases the total number of joints requiring maintenance over the pavement life.

How do you account for monsoon rainfall in the pavement design?

Monsoon precipitation in Tempe, though brief, can deliver high-intensity rainfall that saturates the upper subgrade if drainage is inadequate. Our designs include a sensitivity analysis that reduces the effective modulus of subgrade reaction by 30 to 50 percent to simulate wet-season conditions, and we specify a permeable drainage layer or edge drains with positive outlet where the subgrade consists of moisture-sensitive clay. Joint sealing and a properly graded subbase are the first lines of defense against water-related distress.
